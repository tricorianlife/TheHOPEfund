Understanding your diagnosis.

A diagnosis of an upper GI or HPB cancer raises more questions than answers. This page explains, in plain language, what these cancers are, how they are treated, and why genetic testing may matter for you or someone you love. Written for patients and families, and reviewed by our surgeons.

What do “upper GI” and “HPB” actually mean?

These are shorthand terms your care team will use often. Knowing what they refer to makes every appointment easier to follow.

Upper GI (upper gastrointestinal) refers to the upper part of the digestive system: the oesophagus (the food pipe), the stomach, and the first part of the small bowel (the duodenum). Food travels down the oesophagus, is broken down in the stomach, and passes into the duodenum, where digestion continues.

HPB stands for hepato-pancreato-biliary. It covers three organs that work closely together to digest food:

Hepato means the liver, the body’s chemical factory. Pancreato means the pancreas, which produces digestive enzymes and hormones like insulin. Biliary refers to the bile ducts and gallbladder, the drainage network that carries bile from the liver and gallbladder into the bowel to help digest fat.

These organs sit close together and share a blood supply and drainage routes, which is why cancers in this region are grouped, studied and operated on together by specialist HPB and upper GI surgeons. In fact, the branching bile-duct network is so tree-like that surgeons call it the biliary tree, the very shape that inspired our logo.

Know your diagnosis

The cancers we focus on.

Each of these begins in a different organ, but they share one thing: they are among the hardest cancers to detect early, which is why research and specialist care matter so much.

Oesophagus

Oesophageal cancer

Begins in the lining of the food pipe. The two main types are adenocarcinoma (often linked to long-standing reflux) and squamous cell carcinoma. Early changes can be subtle, such as difficulty swallowing or persistent heartburn.

Also called: cancer of the gullet, oesophageal adenocarcinoma

Stomach

Stomach (gastric) cancer

Begins in the stomach, most often in the inner lining. Symptoms such as indigestion, feeling full quickly or unexplained weight loss are easy to mistake for everyday complaints, which is why it is often found at a later stage.

Also called: gastric cancer, gastric adenocarcinoma

Pancreas

Pancreatic cancer

Begins in the pancreas, most commonly in the ducts that carry digestive enzymes. It tends to grow quietly and spread early, so research into earlier detection, such as our pancreatic cyst registry, is a priority.

Also called: pancreatic adenocarcinoma, pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma

Liver

Liver cancer

Primary liver cancer begins in the liver itself, most often in people with long-standing liver disease or cirrhosis. The liver can also be a site where cancers from elsewhere spread, which your team will distinguish carefully.

Also called: hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)

Bile ducts & gallbladder

Biliary tract cancer

Begins in the bile ducts (cholangiocarcinoma) or the gallbladder. These cancers can block the flow of bile, causing jaundice, a yellowing of the skin and eyes, which is often the first noticeable sign.

Also called: cholangiocarcinoma, gallbladder cancer

Where the oesophagus meets the stomach

Gastro-oesophageal junction cancer

Begins where the oesophagus joins the stomach, an area often abbreviated as the GOJ or OG junction. It is treated by the same specialist teams as oesophageal and stomach cancers.

Also called: GOJ cancer, junctional adenocarcinoma

How care is planned

How these cancers are treated.

Treatment is decided by a multidisciplinary team, surgeons, oncologists, radiologists and nurses reviewing your case together. The plan depends on the cancer type, its stage, your overall health, and increasingly, the genetic profile of the tumour.

SurgeryOften the cornerstone

For many upper GI and HPB cancers, an operation to remove the tumour offers the best chance of cure. These are some of the most complex operations in surgery, which is why they are best done by high-volume specialist teams. Depending on the cancer, this may involve removing part of the oesophagus, stomach, pancreas, liver or bile ducts.

ChemotherapyMedicine that travels

Anti-cancer drugs that circulate through the whole body. Chemotherapy may be given before surgery to shrink a tumour (neoadjuvant), after surgery to reduce the risk of return (adjuvant), or as the main treatment when surgery is not suitable.

RadiotherapyPrecise energy

High-energy beams targeted at the tumour, sometimes combined with chemotherapy (chemoradiation). It is used most often for oesophageal and some stomach and biliary cancers, either to shrink a tumour before surgery or to control symptoms.

Targeted therapyAttacking specific weaknesses

Drugs that block specific molecules a cancer needs to grow, for example the HER2 protein in some stomach and junction cancers. These only work if the tumour carries the target, which is why testing the tumour first matters.

ImmunotherapyUnleashing your immune system

Treatments that help your own immune system recognise and attack cancer cells. They have changed outcomes for several upper GI cancers, particularly tumours that show specific markers such as MSI-H or high PD-L1.

Many patients receive a combination of these, and clinical trials may offer access to the next generation of treatments. Ask your team whether a trial is appropriate for you.

A question worth asking

Genetic testing and tumour profiling.

One of the most important questions in modern cancer care: has my tumour been tested, and could the result change my treatment?

Every cancer carries changes, or mutations, in its DNA. Some of these changes drive the cancer to grow, and some of them can be targeted by specific drugs. Tumour profiling (sometimes called molecular or genomic testing) looks for these changes in a sample of the tumour, usually taken at biopsy or surgery.

This is different from testing the DNA you were born with, though both can matter. Profiling the tumour helps match you to targeted or immune treatments. Testing your inherited genes can reveal whether a cancer runs in a family and whether relatives should be screened.

Why it can change your care

Matching the treatment to the tumour.

For several upper GI and HPB cancers, a specific test result can open the door to a treatment that would otherwise not be offered. Examples your team may discuss include:

1

HER2 testing in stomach and junction cancers

Tumours that overproduce the HER2 protein may respond to targeted drugs such as trastuzumab, added to chemotherapy. HER2 testing is now standard for advanced stomach and gastro-oesophageal junction cancers.

2

MSI and mismatch repair testing

Tumours described as MSI-H or mismatch repair deficient have a particular weakness that immunotherapy can exploit. This test is increasingly done across upper GI cancers because a positive result can make immunotherapy an option.

3

BRCA testing in pancreatic cancer

Some pancreatic cancers carry a BRCA1 or BRCA2 change, either inherited or found in the tumour. For selected patients, this can open the door to a class of targeted drugs called PARP inhibitors.

4

FGFR2 and IDH1 in bile duct cancer

Cholangiocarcinoma is one of the cancers where tumour profiling has moved fastest. Changes in the FGFR2 or IDH1 genes can, in some cases, be matched to targeted therapies available through trials or special access.

5

Broader panels and clinical trials

Comprehensive genomic profiling looks at many genes at once and can reveal rarer targets, such as NTRK fusions, that have treatments approved across cancer types. Results can also match you to clinical trials.

Not every tumour has a targetable change, and not every result leads to a different treatment. But testing is the only way to know, and it is a reasonable and increasingly routine thing to ask about.

Take this with you

Questions to ask your oncologist.

Appointments move quickly. Writing questions down beforehand helps you leave with the answers you need. These are a good starting point for a conversation about genetic testing and your options.

About testing

Start the conversation.

  • Has my tumour been tested for biomarkers such as HER2, MSI or mismatch repair status?
  • Would comprehensive genomic profiling of my tumour be useful in my case?
  • Are there any inherited genetic tests I or my family should consider, such as BRCA?
  • If a targetable change is found, what treatments or clinical trials could it open up?
  • How would the test result change my treatment plan, and when would we know it?

And about your care more broadly:

  • What is the goal of my treatment: cure, control, or symptom relief?
  • Is my case being reviewed by a multidisciplinary team, and is surgery an option?
  • Are there clinical trials I should know about?
  • Who do I contact between appointments if I have new symptoms or questions?
ⓘ

A note on this information. This page is general education, not medical advice. Every cancer and every person is different. Always discuss your diagnosis, testing and treatment options with your own care team, who know the details of your case. If you are unsure whether genetic testing is right for you, ask your oncologist directly.

For surgeons, nurses & trainees

Education for the profession.

Education at The HOPE Fund runs in both directions. As well as informing patients, we invest in training the next generation of upper GI and HPB surgeons and the wider care team.

Many of our research projects run in partnership with St Vincent’s Hospital, a teaching hospital where registrars on accredited surgical training programs with RACS are supervised and trained.

Surgical fellowships with global reach.

The HOPE Fund supports surgical training through fellowships, 18-month programs which provide specialty skills and contribute to higher global standards in surgical practice. Dr Keith Dindi, from rural Kenya, is one of the first graduates and is qualified as both a general and cardiothoracic surgeon.

To facilitate training, we work with OESO to ensure worldwide collaboration and high quality training. With Melbourne as a key centre to offer training, the program provides Dr Dindi with the necessary skillsets through six months of rotations in cardiac surgery, thoracic surgery and general / vascular surgery.

A scholarship that came home.

With our support, surgeon Mr Matthew Read completed a fellowship with a focus on robotic surgery at the University Medical Center in Utrecht, the Netherlands, and continued his research into oesophageal cancer. Since returning to St Vincent’s as a consultant surgeon, Matthew has established key international collaborations and furthered local research interests.
