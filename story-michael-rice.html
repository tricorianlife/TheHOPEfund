A new lease on life.

Two years after surgery, oesophageal cancer survivor Michael Rice is back running and going to the gym. After decades of caring for others, his commitment to fitness and gratitude is truly inspirational.

Michael Rice with his partner Linda
A story of hope

Michael’s new lease on life

“I had been having discomfort swallowing food for two to three weeks and the discomfort was getting worse. I have been treated for several years for oesophageal reflux and, having retired from 40 years working as a registered nurse, was aware of the damage that gastric acid can have on the oesophagus, not to mention I was a smoker for 25 years. My GP referred me for a gastroscopy on the 18th of November 2020, which found a large growth at the junction of my stomach and oesophagus.”

“Hi, I’m Michael Rice, a fit and healthy 73 year old. Always been a jogger. Two years prior to this diagnosis my daughter and I completed the Chicago Marathon together. I have also been conscious of maintaining a healthy diet, apart from the occasional pitfalls!”

“I think I knew that the gastroscopy was going to reveal a growth, a full thickness adenocarcinoma, so I was not really in shock with the news. In fact my reaction was one of, well, what happens next? Let’s get on with what needs to be done. Dr Chamara Basnayake, who had performed the gastroscopy, was already writing two referrals immediately after informing me of his findings: a CT of the chest, abdomen and pelvis for the following day.”

“The other referral was to a surgeon at St Vincent’s Hospital, Mr Matthew Read. His comment was that ‘if it was my mother, this is the surgeon I would be referring her to.’”

“After 40 years in nursing, I’d seen aspects of the public health system at its worst and at its best, but mostly at its best, and that’s the experience I had with my predicament.”Michael Rice

By the end of November, Michael had a PET scan, laparoscopy, further gastroscopy and a lung function test to see if he was fit enough for surgery. Investigations found some small lymph node involvement around the site, but no metastases. He would need chemotherapy and radiation to shrink the tumour, followed by surgery. The chemotherapy and radiation took place at Frankston Hospital, his last place of employment, and Frankston Private Hospital, a considerate arrangement as he lives on the Mornington Peninsula, saving him a daily drive to St Vincent’s.

Chemo and radiation lasted a month and, apart from some small discomfort with nausea and loss of appetite, he was okay. The good news was that the tumour had shrunk and there was still no spread.

“On the 18th of March 2021 I woke up in Intensive Care after an 8 to 10 hour surgical procedure the day before. I remember smiling to myself and yelling out loudly in my mind, ‘I’m alive!’”

After being discharged seven days after his operation, the roller coaster ride commenced: pain, nausea, severe abdominal discomfort when eating, gastric reflux, insomnia and an 8kg weight loss. He developed an infection at the feeding tube site requiring a brief admission for antibiotics. He couldn’t walk to the bathroom without being short of breath. For someone used to running at least 6km a day, this left him devastated. He often found himself in tears with depression, wondering if it was all worthwhile.

“Mr Read had said to me that post-op would not be an easy journey and it would take a year or longer before I was back to anything near normal. My partner Linda and I set about making plans for my progress.”Michael Rice

Michael knew he had to focus or be overwhelmed. His GP prescribed an antidepressant, he resumed regularly taking his analgesia with a weaning plan, experimented with sleeping positions to relieve his reflux and started having smaller meals more often.

“It has been just over two years now since my surgery and I am back running and going to the gym, enjoying food with some small changes to how and what I eat. Despite COVID, the public health system, and by that I mean the surgeons, oncologists, nurses, physiotherapists, dieticians, anaesthetists, radiotherapists and cleaners, showed their true colours. But most of all my lovely partner Linda, who was there when I needed her most. Thank you!”
